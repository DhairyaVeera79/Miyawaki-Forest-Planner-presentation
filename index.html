SRLC AI Portfolio • Project 4

Miyawaki Forest Planner

A practical and mission-aligned platform to plan, execute, monitor, and communicate Miyawaki afforestation outcomes with discipline, transparency, and long-term ecological care.

Core Build Target: 31 August 2026 Offering Readiness: 26 September 2026

The problem you are solving

Why SRLC needs this now

  • Afforestation seva has strong intent, but execution can become fragmented across locations.
  • As project volume grows, organizational memory must shift from ad-hoc updates to durable records.
  • Leadership, sevaks, donors, and beneficiaries require transparent, evidence-backed impact pathways.
  • Dates are fixed, so execution quality and phased delivery discipline are essential.

Current pain points

  • Site planning is often manual, with inconsistent digital workflows.
  • Species selection and Miyawaki layer balancing vary across teams.
  • Sapling quantity, procurement buffers, and inventory tracking are not standardized end-to-end.
  • Field SOPs are not always available as one guided sequence for new volunteers.
  • Monitoring cadence, survival tracking, and evidence quality are uneven.
  • Impact communication is harder without verified milestone and media trails.

Impact at stake if gaps continue

Reduced survival outcomes Higher rework and coordination overhead Lower governance confidence Weaker donor and beneficiary reporting readiness

The proposed solution

End-to-end product vision

Deliver a field-friendly platform that enables teams to define sites, generate Miyawaki-native plans, operationalize procurement, run SOP-driven execution, monitor growth, and publish approved impact narratives.

  • India-first native species planning with curated references and expert override.
  • Balanced four-layer Miyawaki planning: canopy, tree, sub-tree, and shrub.
  • Role-based governance across C1/C2/C3/C4 stakeholders, advisors, and admins.

Core modules in scope

  • Site planner: polygon draw/re-draw, area, centroid, coordinates, region detection, audit history.
  • Ecological planning: native shortlist by geography, layer mapping, optional tags, availability signal.
  • Quantity and procurement: density-controlled counts, buffer logic, PO-ready vendor output, IMS-lite.
  • Layout blueprint: spacing rules, geometry options, density cap enforcement, visual brief summary.
  • SOP and readiness: checklists, natural-input-first guidance, quick orientation mode.
  • Monitoring and dashboard: cadence reminders, photo capture points, risk flags, leadership rollups.

Phase 3 · Outcomes and Governance

  5. Auditable records for planning and publishing decisions

Architecture and intelligence principles

User Interfaces

Field mobile workflows, coordinator console, leadership dashboard, approved storytelling views.

Core Services

Site planner, species engine, layout and SOP modules, monitoring, reporting, role-based governance.

Data and AI Governance

Structured entities, media pipeline, analytics, advisory AI with confidence and mandatory human review.

AI is assistive, transparent, and overrideable No autonomous irreversible decisions External communication requires approved data Disease and maturity conclusions require expert validation

Core data foundation

The platform tracks a complete record across projects, site polygons, species catalog entries, layer assignments, quantity plans, procurement batches, purchase orders, inventory ledgers, SOP tasks, monitoring entries, media assets, forecasts, risk flags, and impact stories.

Analytics outcomes

  • Survival trends over time and species-level performance visibility.
  • Delay and dropout risk indicators with phase forecast confidence.
  • Project-to-project compliance comparison for leadership oversight.
  • Contribution-to-impact summaries for donor communication quality.

Key technical or operational dependencies

Operational dependencies

  • Ecological validator network for species and plan approval checkpoints.
  • Geography-wise nursery and vendor ecosystem mapping.
  • SOP ownership, training accountability, and steady field participation models.
  • Consistent monitoring commitment and escalation discipline by project teams.

Data dependencies

  • Regional native species references and lifecycle information.
  • Climate and soil context where available.
  • Historical plantation outcomes for baseline calibration.
  • High-quality field media and metadata discipline across teams.

Platform dependencies

  • Authentication and role-based access controls.
  • Reliable storage and media pipeline.
  • Notification and reminder channels.
  • Reporting stack for project and portfolio analytics.

Risk controls and mitigations

  • Species mismatch risk reduced by mandatory expert review before plan lock.
  • Monitoring inconsistency reduced through reminders, accountability, and escalation views.
  • AI over-reliance reduced through advisory-only behavior and human sign-off.
  • Volunteer turnover managed through SOP continuity and persistent project memory.
  • Reporting inaccuracies prevented by publishing only approved and verified data.

Non-functional readiness requirements

  • Usability for field conditions with minimal friction.
  • Reliability under intermittent connectivity patterns where feasible.
  • Security, auditability, and governance for key decisions.
  • Scalability from pilot execution to multi-site portfolios.
  • Localization-ready terminology and instruction adaptability.

Governance, ethics, and communication standards

  • Use respectful, non-commercial mission language for all ecological communication.
  • Maintain dignified storytelling for beneficiaries and local communities.
  • Avoid extractive narrative framing in impact representation.
  • Preserve clear human accountability in ecological and publishing decisions.
